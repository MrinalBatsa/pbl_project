Explainability for Credit Card Default Risk Prediction

"Can historical repayment behavior, credit utilization patterns, and demographic attributes reliably predict credit default risk while ensuring interpretability and transparency using Explainable AI techniques?"

Problem & Research Motivation

Credit default risk is a critical issue in the banking sector. Financial institutions lose billions annually due to unpaid obligations. Traditional rule-based and linear credit scoring systems lack flexibility for complex financial patterns.

Machine learning improves predictive power but often lacks interpretability. This project integrates Random Forest modeling with SHAP explainability to provide transparent and reliable risk assessment.

Methodology

The project uses the UCI Default of Credit Card Clients dataset consisting of 30,000 samples and 23 financial and demographic features. The target variable is binary, indicating whether a client defaults in the next month. Data preprocessing involved removal of the ID column, correction of categorical encodings, and validation of feature consistency. The dataset was split using an 80/20 stratified train-test approach to preserve class imbalance. Since only about 22% of clients default, the dataset is imbalanced. To address this, class_weight='balanced' was applied to ensure the model gives higher importance to minority class samples.

A Random Forest classifier with 300 trees and controlled depth was trained to capture non-linear relationships and reduce overfitting through ensemble learning. Performance evaluation included Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1 Score, and ROC-AUC to assess both overall correctness and minority class detection. Finally, SHAP explainability techniques were applied to interpret how individual features influence predictions at both global and local levels.
